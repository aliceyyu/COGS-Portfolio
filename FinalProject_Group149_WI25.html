Abstract¶

This project investigated the frequency and severity of commercial aviation safety incidents since the 1980s to assess how the causes—human error, mechanical error, and weather—have changed over time. With recent news coverage in early 2025 highlighting airplane emergencies, examining aviation safety trends has become increasingly significant, as well as determining whether technological advances have reduced mechanical problems while human error remains as a persistent factor. We used datasets from the NTSB Accident Investigation Database and the Kaggle Airplane Crashes dataset to identify trends, categorize incidents, and compare fatality rates. Our findings showed that human error continues to be the dominant cause of events, and mechanical errors have steadily decreased since 2012. Additionally, adverse weather conditions persist in contributing to a substantial number of commercial airplane crashes, maintaining a steady pattern of frequency across the analyzed timespan. The results emphasize the need for continued attention to human factors and maintaining the rise in quality of mechanical components in aviation safety.

Research Question¶

How do the frequency and severity of global commercial aircraft safety incidents (including accidents, serious incidents, and mechanical failures) vary since the 1980s?

Sub-question:

What proportion of commercial aircraft safety incidents can be attributed to human error versus mechanical failure, and how have these trends evolved over time?

Background and Prior Work¶

The field of commercial aviation safety has achieved multiple improvements over the recent decades, establishing planes as among the safest ways of transportation worldwide. Recent research has demonstrated that commercial air travel continues to become significantly safer, with the risk of fatality dropping to approximately 1 per every 13.7 million passenger boardings during the 2018–2022 period 1. The evolution of commercial aviation safety follows a consistent pattern of improvement, particularly evident in fatal accident rates 2. In the early 1970s, there were approximately 6 fatal accidents per million flights. These numbers have been on a downward trend since then. MIT researchers have categorized countries into three distinct tiers based on their safety performance: First Tier, with approximately 1 death per 80 million boardings; 2nd Tier, with safety metrics similar to the First Tier (~1 per 80 million), and 3rd Tier, which have a fatality rate 36.5 times higher than the top tier. The United States belongs in the first tier along with countries Australia, Canada, and Japan 1. However, even if the incidents have decreased over time, they still continue to occur and threaten the safety of commercial aviation, remaining a critical concern for the global travel industry. Understanding the factors behind safety incidents can help improve flight safety. Our research questions include the exploration of topics about the frequency and severity of these incidents and how they have evolved since the 21st century. We also want to investigate which aircraft components are most frequently involved and examine trends in incidents caused by human error versus mechanical failure to see if there are any visual patterns.

A lot of the annual reduction in accidents can be owed to technological advancements. For example, collision avoidance systems and advanced monitoring technologies have played a crucial role in improving safety metrics. However, human factors continue to be a critical area of focus. Several authoritative databases track aviation safety globally; these sources provide standardized metrics, enabling consistent tracking of safety trends over time. Numerous studies explore aviation safety using large datasets, including the Aviation Safety Network (ASN) and National Transportation Safety Board (NTSB) databases, which track global accidents and incidents 3. For example, the Aviation Safety Reporting System (ASRS) collects reports from aviation professionals and has been crucial in identifying human factors and mechanical issues in incidents.

Despite remarkable progress, challenges remain, particularly in addressing persistent human error factors, implementing emerging technologies effectively, and maintaining consistent global safety standards. In this project, we aim to perform a comprehensive data analysis to explore safety incident trends since 2000, examine the relationship between component failures and the severity of accidents, and analyze the distinct roles that human and mechanical factors play in influencing safety outcomes. In data science, predictive modeling has been used to forecast accident rates based on weather, aircraft models, and other factors. More recently, machine learning techniques have helped uncover patterns in large datasets, while natural language processing (NLP) has been used to analyze incident reports. Notable projects like the Global Aviation Safety Network (GASN) apply advanced analytics to identify trends and improve aviation safety. Our research builds on these efforts, we aim to apply statistical analysis and data visualization to existing datasets, contributing insights into how incidents are evolving and which factors are most influential.

Hypothesis¶

Our hypothesis is that there has been a decline in mechanical failures throughout time due to the advancements in technology. Additionally, we predict that there has been no change in the rate of plane crashes caused by pilot error. Finally, we predict that there has been no change in crashes caused by weather due to weather being a natural component which people cannot control for.

Dataset #1

  • Dataset Name: NTSB Accident Investigation Database
  • Link to the dataset: https://www.ntsb.gov/Pages/ResultsV2.aspx?queryId=a0ca4805-de9d-4653-ac57-2b2af7027a5c
  • Number of observations: 2626
  • Total number of variables in the dataset: 38
  • Number of variables used: 11

This is a dataset from the National Transportation Safety Board on commercial airplane flight crashes from 1982 to 2025. The 11 variables in the dataset that are important for our analysis are:

  • EventDate (datetime64): date of the crash
  • HighestInjuryLevel (string): whether the highest injury level of people onboard was minor, serious, or fatal
  • FatalInjuryCount (int64): number of fatal injuries
  • SeriousInjuryCount (int64): number of serious injuries (requiring hospitalization over 48 hours, breaking bones, burns, etc)
  • MinorInjuryCount (int64): number of minor injuries
  • ProbableCause(string): cause of the crash
  • Make (string): make of the airplane that crashed
  • Model (string): model of the airplane that crashed
  • AirCraftDamage (string): whether the airplane was damaged seriously, minorly, or destroyed
  • WeatherCondition (string): weather condition during the flight, where IMC indicates poor visibility resulting in pilot utilizing flight instruments
  • Operator (string): flight airline

Dataset #2

  • Dataset Name: Kaggle Airplane Crashes and Fatalities
  • Link to the dataset: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/thedevastator/airplane-crashes-and-fatalities
  • Number of observations: 5267
  • Total number of variables: 13
  • Number of variables used: 5

This dataset contains information on plane crashes from various types of flights from 1908 to 2009. We are going to use the data starting from 1982 to align with our first dataset. Through this data, we are able to map out the fatality rate over time. To do this we will calculate the fatality proportion for each crash by dividing the number of fatalities by the number abroad for the percentages. Finally, we will define criteria for categories of the percentages.

The 5 important variables in this dataset are:

  • Date (datetime64): date of the crash
  • Operator (string): flight airline
  • Aboard (int64): total number of people on the plane
  • Fatalities (int64): Number of people aboard who died
  • Summary (string): cause of the crash

For our analysis, we analyzed two separate datasets: the NTSB dataset and the Kaggle dataset. Due to differences in variables, potential overlap in reported crashes, and varying time periods covered, we did not merge the datasets. Our primary focus is on the NTSB dataset. The Kaggle dataset will serve as a supporting dataset for comparison to the findings of the NTSB data. We will compare the general trend of fatal crashes over time for both datasets by plotting them side by side, allowing us to visually assess if they align. This will help confirm whether the NTSB data is consistent with another large dataset of commercial airplane crashes, providing additional confidence in its validity. Specifically, the Kaggle Airplane Crashes and Fatalities dataset will be used to visualize the overall trend of fatal crashes over time from 1982, when the NTSB data begins, up until 2009, when the Kaggle data ends. We will see how this trend has carried on or changed to the present using the NTSB data set (our main dataset) which has data up until 2025.

def clean_text(text):
    """
    Cleans the text by removing punctuation and splitting it into words. Converts everything to lowercase.
    """
    punctuation_chars = ".,!?;:'\"()[]{}<>-/_&"
    
    # Replace punctuation with spaces
    for char in punctuation_chars:
        text = text.replace(char, ' ')
    
    # Convert text to lowercase and split into words
    words = text.lower().split()
    return words

def process_row(text, stop_words):
    """
    Processes a single row of text by cleaning and filtering out stop words. Returns a list of words.
    """
    # Clean the text and split it into words
    words = clean_text(text)
    
    # Filter out stop words
    filtered_words = [word for word in words if word not in stop_words]
    
    return filtered_words

def count_top_words(csv_file, column_name, top_n):
    """
    Reads CSV file row by row, processes the specified column to count word frequencies
    excluding stop words, and returns the top N most common words.
    The results will be used in categorizing incident causes for data analysis.
    """
    # Initialize a Counter to count word frequencies
    word_counts = Counter()
    
    # Clean dataset using previously-defined drop_cols function
    cleaned = drop_cols(commercialflights)
    
    for text in commercialflights[column_name].dropna():
        filtered_words = process_row(str(text), stop_words)
        word_counts.update(filtered_words)
    
    # Get the top N most common words
    most_common_words = word_counts.most_common(top_n)
    
    return most_common_words

def drop_specific_operators(df):
    keywords = ["military", "mail", "air taxi"]
    df = df.dropna(subset=["Operator"])  # Ensure 'Operator' column has no NaN values
    pattern = '|'.join(keywords)  # Create a regex pattern from keywords
    filtered_df = df[~df["Operator"].str.contains(pattern, case=False, na=False)] #Case-insensitive filtering
    return filtered_df

def categorize_incident(text):
    """
    Categorizes the incident into Weather, Human Error, or Mechanical Error
    based on keyword occurrences in the processed probable cause text.
    """

    # The top 30 words were categorized into the three categories below manually
    weather_keywords = {'turbulence', 'air', 'conditions', 'weather', 'bird'} # Where 'bird' was noticed after manually looking at dataset
    human_mech_incidentsor_keywords = {'crew', 'captain', 'pilot', 'attendant', 'personnel', 'operator', 'driver', 'traffic', 'passenger', 'service', 'decision', 'officer'}
    mechanical_error_keywords = {'engine', 'gear', 'maintenance', 'system', 'fuel', 'controller', 'separation', 'fire', 'pressure', 'hydraulic', 'fan', 'nose', 'power',
                                'blade', 'turbine'}
    
    words = set(text.split())  # Convert text into a set of words for quick lookup
    
    if words & weather_keywords:
        return 'Weather'
    elif words & human_mech_incidentsor_keywords:
        return 'Human error'
    elif words & mechanical_error_keywords:
        return 'Mechanical error'
    else:
        return 'Other'  # Default category if no keywords match

Ethics & Privacy¶

Dataset #1 comes from the National Transportation Safety Board, which is a federal agency that investigates U.S. aviation accidents. Each accident observation has a docket report linked to it which contains flight crew members’ full names and ages. As this personally identifiable information is not relevant to our analysis of airplane crashes, we did not include the data column with the links to the reports. Additionally, we removed the NTSB identification numbers for the incidents so that the reports can not be easily accessed. However, since all information on NTSB crashes is publicly available, we acknowledge that the names of flight crew members could still be accessed. Our second dataset, accessed from Kaggle, does not contain any personally identifiable information.

An additional privacy concern regarding our datasets includes the handling of data on the number of injuries and fatalities in plane crashes, which, in combination with the date and airline operator of the crashes, could possibly be linked to certain passengers or crew members. We deem the privacy risk to be low, in part due to our choice to remove the locations of the crashes.

A concern of possible bias we identify is the current presence of strong public opinions regarding the safety of particular U.S. airlines. This has followed the recent significant media attention towards airplane crashes, driving a sentiment of public fear that plane crashes have been increasing. Additionally, our objective analysis of plane crashes across different airlines does not intend to direct distrust or any otherwise negative publicity towards any commercial airline, but rather is concerned with investigating flight accidents from a safety standpoint.

Discussion and Conclusion¶

This project explored how the trends in global commercial aircraft accidents have changed from the 1980's until now. We hypothesized that there has been a decline in mechanical failures over time as a result of advancements in technology. Secondly, we predicted that there has not been a change in the rate of plane crashes caused by pilot error. Thirdly, we hypothesized that there has been no change in crashes due to weather due to storms and low visibility out of human control consistently posing safety risks.

Our graphs indicate that the frequency of aviation accidents in general have decreased over time. Looking specifically at Graphs 2 and 3, the totals are going down overall, as explained in our analysis. Our hypothesis that the human error crash rate would stay the same is not supported. For the mechanical and weather parts of our hypothesis, our hypothesis was supported, since there has been a decrease over time for mechanical incidents and a steady trend for weather incidents.

Furthermore, a subquestion we were interested in answering was how the frequency and makeup of overall flights compares between mechanical failures and human errors, and if this comparison has been maintained or changed over time. From our analysis, it appears that crashes caused by human errors are more common than those caused by mechanical errors in every year of our dataset. This is most prominently shown in Graph 3. Additionally, Graph 3 shows that trends for both are decreasing over time. While incidents still occur in both categories, there is a lower numnber overall compared to the start of the dataset.

A potential explanation of why crashes due to human error have decreased is that perhaps operating guidelines for planes have been consistently updated to increase effectiveness of procedures leading to fewer human errors overall. Another explanation may be that advancements in aviation technology have lead to human errors being correctable or less serious in that they do not result as crashes as often. Explaining the decrease in mechanical errors and the consistent occurence of crashes due to adverse weather, our reasoning underlying our hypothesis provides a reasonable explanation. That is, that better manufacturing and technology has lead to increased reliability and durability of the mechanical components of airplanes, and that weather remains a constant issue to deal with when flying.

Ultimately, although public sentiment as of early 2025 reflects an increased doubt in the safety of flying due to sequential airplane crashes across the U.S. receiving extensive media attention, our findings show that that fear is unfounded since the rates of yearly crashes has been decreasing.

One limitation of our project is that our approach to categorizing the probable causes of the commercial flights into our categories of interest resulted in the specificity of details being reduced. This led to information on the types of human errors, weather events, and part failures not being considered. Additionally, it resulted in a substantial number of crashes being categorized as other.

Future research is needed to explore what types of human errors, mechanical failures, and weather conditions, tend to contribute to more fatal crashes, and which types happen most overall. Looking more into this will allow more attention to be directed to aspects of commercial aviation that need more development so as to reduce the rate and severity of aviation accidents and to continue to make flying safer overall.
